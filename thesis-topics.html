MSc Thesis · Machine Learning

Machine Learning for Robust and Interpretable Physiological Monitoring in Anaesthesia

Modern anaesthesia generates large volumes of high-resolution physiological data — ECG, arterial blood pressure, oxygen saturation, respiratory signals, anaesthetic depth indices, drug administration records, and perioperative outcomes. Open datasets such as VitalDB provide an unprecedented opportunity to develop ML methods for intraoperative monitoring, early warning systems, and clinical decision support. Several research directions are available depending on student interests.

→ View full description on Canvas
Objectives & Alternative Topics

Alternative 1 — Predicting anaesthetic depth from physiological signals

Develop models that estimate anaesthetic depth or BIS-related states from multimodal physiological recordings. The work will compare classical signal processing, interpretable ML, and deep learning to better understand the physiological determinants of anaesthetic depth.

Alternative 2 — Personalised anaesthesia monitoring

Investigate whether patient-specific and adaptive ML models can improve prediction of physiological deterioration during surgery, accounting for inter-patient variability in physiological responses to anaesthesia, surgical stimulation, and pharmacological interventions.

Alternative 3 — Early prediction of intraoperative hypotension

Develop ML models capable of predicting hypotensive episodes several minutes before onset using multimodal physiological waveforms, vital signs, drug administration data, and patient characteristics — providing clinically useful early warning systems.

Alternative 4 — Data quality assessment and artefact detection

Create automated methods for identifying artefacts, sensor failures, calibration problems, missing-data patterns, and unreliable waveform segments in large-scale physiological datasets, improving both research reproducibility and clinical AI reliability.

Methodology

The project will primarily use the VitalDB dataset, which contains thousands of surgical cases with synchronised physiological waveforms, numerical monitoring variables, laboratory measurements, medication administration records, and perioperative clinical information.

Depending on the selected direction, the student will preprocess high-resolution signals (ECG, arterial blood pressure, plethysmography, capnography, SpO₂, anaesthesia monitoring parameters), define clinically relevant prediction tasks, and develop appropriate ML solutions.

Potential methods include signal processing, feature engineering, gradient boosting, temporal convolutional networks, RNNs, transformers, representation learning, anomaly detection, and explainable AI. Particular emphasis will be placed on model robustness, interpretability, patient-level validation, uncertainty estimation, and clinically meaningful evaluation.

Expected Outputs

A reproducible ML pipeline for analysing large-scale physiological monitoring data, together with a quantitative evaluation of predictive or diagnostic models for a clinically relevant perioperative task. Expected deliverables include a well-documented codebase, benchmark comparisons of modelling approaches, analysis of clinically relevant physiological features, and recommendations for reliable AI-based monitoring. Results may be suitable for conference or journal publication.

MSc Thesis · Machine Learning

Beyond Affect Labels: Evaluating and Improving Emotion Annotation Strategies for Physiological Computing

Affective computing systems rely on labelled physiological datasets to train emotion recognition models. However, the quality of the annotation process itself often has a larger impact on model performance than the choice of algorithm. Existing datasets use a wide variety of labelling approaches — protocol-based labels, continuous self-reporting, post-hoc questionnaires, ecological momentary assessments — yet little work has systematically investigated which strategies produce labels most consistent with the underlying physiological signals.

→ View full description on Canvas
Objective

Extend and improve a recently proposed affect-label validation framework for physiological datasets. The primary objective is to compare different annotation methodologies and determine which types of labels exhibit the strongest correspondence with physiological signals.

As a secondary objective, the student may investigate novel validation metrics and alternative labelling strategies, including personalised labels, consensus-based annotations, temporal smoothing, uncertainty-aware labelling, ordinal labels, and different discretisation strategies. The work may also explore whether certain annotation schemes systematically overestimate the emotional information contained in physiological signals.

Methodology & Datasets

The project will analyse multiple affective computing datasets collected using different annotation methodologies and emotional elicitation protocols. Datasets in italics are already part of prior work:

  • WESAD — protocol-based labels assigned by experimental design
  • CASE — continuous real-time valence/arousal annotations
  • DREAMER — post-hoc SAM questionnaires
  • DEAP — valence, arousal, dominance and liking ratings
  • MAHNOB-HCI — multimodal recordings with self-assessment labels
  • SWELL-KW — stress and user modelling dataset

The work will include: (1) implementation and extension of the existing validation framework, (2) development of additional label-quality metrics, (3) comparison of annotation schemes within and across datasets, (4) analysis of temporal alignment between labels and physiological signals, and (5) evidence-based recommendations for affective dataset design.

Expected Outputs

An extended framework for evaluating affective label quality, a systematic comparison of annotation methodologies, and practical recommendations for researchers collecting new affective datasets. Deliverables include a reproducible benchmarking framework and a well-documented software pipeline. Results may be suitable for conference or journal publication.

MSc Thesis · Internship · Biomedical AI

Joint Embedding Learning of Cardiac MRI & Radio-Frequency Sensing

Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of death worldwide. Imaging modalities such as echocardiography and cardiac MRI are the gold standard for measuring mechanical cardiac biomarkers, yet they are limited to single-point, hospital-based measurements. PrecorDx and UMC Utrecht have developed a promising non-invasive technique called radio-frequency sensing (RFS), which uses compact, on-body RF antennas to track cardiac activity outside hospital settings and enable longitudinal monitoring. This project explores the use of foundation models (FM) and contrastive learning strategies to discover a shared embedding space between cardiac MR data and RF signals — opening a path toward continuous, home-based cardiovascular assessment.

ⓘ Paid internship — This assignment is offered as a compensated internship position hosted at Utrecht Science Park (in-person / hybrid), in collaboration with UMC Utrecht and PrecorDx. Duration: approximately 6 months, start date flexible. The student will receive dedicated mentorship from experienced UMCU researchers and the PrecorDx team.

Objective

Develop and validate a multimodal framework that learns a shared latent embedding space for cardiac MR data and RF signals, leveraging pretrained foundation model encoders and contrastive learning strategies.

Once a shared embedding is established, the goal is twofold. First, from an RF embedding, reconstruct the corresponding MR data so that clinical parameters can be extracted in a conventional way. Second, from the RF embedding, directly predict clinical parameters without first decoding the MR data — enabling efficient, wearable-based cardiovascular monitoring outside the hospital.

Methodology & Tasks

The project follows a multi-modal embedding learning architecture in which separate FM encoders map cardiac MRI and RF sensing data to a shared latent space, which is subsequently used to predict clinical outcome variables. The student will carry out the following core tasks:

  • Develop a Python pipeline for extracting embeddings from cardiac MR and RFS data.
  • Investigate strategies to train and fine-tune FM encoders so that shared cross-modal embeddings between both modalities can be found.
  • Design and run validation experiments using simultaneously acquired, matched MR and RFS data.
  • Build and validate a clinical parameter prediction model operating directly from RF embeddings.
  • Document results and report findings regularly to the research team.

The student will work with simultaneously recorded, matched cardiac MRI and RF sensing data collected at UMC Utrecht. Access to relevant datasets and compute infrastructure will be provided by the host institutions.

Expected Outputs

A reproducible Python pipeline for cross-modal embedding extraction, trained and validated multimodal FM encoders with a shared embedding space, and a validated model for direct clinical parameter prediction from RF embeddings. The student will also produce written documentation and team reports. Results may contribute directly to the development of a breakthrough medical device targeting heart failure patients, and may be suitable for publication.

Student Profile & Contact

Educational background: MSc candidate in Electrical Engineering, Biomedical Engineering, Applied Physics, Artificial Intelligence, or Scientific Computing.

Technical skills: Machine and deep learning, Python, and experience with at least one major framework (PyTorch, TensorFlow, or JAX). Familiarity with biomedical data is valued.

Mindset: Critical, creative, and genuinely interested in biomedical applications; able to work independently as well as within a multidisciplinary team spanning academia and a med-tech startup.

Interested? Send your CV and a short motivation letter to iris.huijben@precordx.com.

MSc Internship · Signal Processing · Biomedical AI

Feasibility Study of a Reduced-Electrode EEG-Based Stroke Detector

Stroke is one of the leading causes of death and disability worldwide, occurring when an artery to the brain becomes blocked (ischemic stroke) or ruptures (hemorrhagic stroke). Time-to-treatment is critical to outcomes — an estimated 1.9 million neurons die every minute following an ischemic stroke — yet timely stroke detection remains a major barrier to effective treatment. Prior studies using EEG for stroke detection in hospital and pre-hospital (ambulance) settings have shown encouraging results. This project builds on that work to assess the feasibility of an improved, faster stroke detector.

ⓘ Paid internship — This assignment is offered as a compensated internship position with Medtronic, hybrid working model (minimum 1 day/week from the office in Maastricht). Duration: approximately 6 months, with a preferred start date of September 2026. Working hours: 20–40 h/week, with flexibility around exam and holiday periods.

Objective

Study the feasibility of an improved stroke detector aimed at reducing stroke detection time. Using existing EEG recordings, the project will determine the minimum number and optimal placement of EEG electrodes needed to reliably and rapidly detect stroke events.

Methodology & Tasks

The project combines signal processing and ML/AI methods to evaluate reduced EEG montages against detection performance. The intern will carry out the following core tasks:

  • Support the process for data acquisition from project partners.
  • Identify the most appropriate method to assess the number and location of EEG electrodes required for reliable detection.
  • Estimate and validate the detector's performance under different electrode configurations.
  • Analyze the EEG data using signal processing and machine learning techniques.
  • Document the activities and results in a technical report.
Expected Outputs

A feasibility assessment of a reduced-electrode EEG stroke detector, including recommended electrode count and placement, an estimate of detector performance under this reduced configuration, and a technical document summarising methods and findings. Results may inform the design of a future point-of-care or pre-hospital stroke detection device.

Student Profile & Contact

Educational background: MSc candidate in Engineering, Neuroscience, Life Science, Data Science, Computer Science, or related fields.

Technical skills: Familiarity with signal processing and ML/AI methods. Previous experience analysing EEG or other electrophysiological signals is a plus.

Mindset: Proactive, analytical, and detail-oriented; able to work effectively in a team, with strong organisational skills. An interest in neuroscience, neurophysiology, and/or medical devices is a plus.

Hosted by Medtronic (Maastricht, hybrid). Interested students should reach out for further application details.
